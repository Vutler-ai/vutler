# User Interview Guide for Requirements Discovery

## Pre-Interview Preparation

**Goals:**
- [ ] Define what you need to learn (specific hypotheses or open exploration?)
- [ ] Identify target user segment
- [ ] Prepare interview guide (this document)
- [ ] Set up recording/note-taking system

**Participant Selection:**
- Current users (understand existing pain)
- Churned users (understand why they left)
- Target users not yet using product (understand barriers)
- Power users (understand advanced needs)

**Logistics:**
- 30-45 minutes per interview
- Record with permission (for later review)
- Have second person take notes if possible
- Offer incentive (gift card, early access, etc.)

## Interview Structure

### Opening (5 min)

**Build rapport and set context:**

"Thanks for taking the time. I'm [Name], working on [Product]. We're trying to understand [problem space] better, and your experience would really help us.

There are no right or wrong answers—we're here to learn from you. Feel free to be brutally honest. This isn't a test, and you won't hurt our feelings.

I'll ask some questions, and it's totally fine to go off on tangents. I might ask 'why' a lot—that's just to understand your thinking better.

Is it okay if I record this? It helps me focus on our conversation instead of taking notes."

### Background (5-10 min)

**Understand context and current state:**

1. "Tell me about your role and what you're working on."
   - [Listen for: responsibilities, team size, tools used]

2. "Walk me through a typical day/week. What are the main things you're trying to get done?"
   - [Listen for: workflows, pain points, priorities]

3. "What tools do you use regularly? Which ones do you love? Which ones frustrate you?"
   - [Listen for: current solutions, workarounds, preferences]

### Problem Discovery (15-20 min)

**Core interview—dig into specific problems:**

4. "Tell me about the last time you [situation related to your product space]."
   - [Listen for: actual behavior, not hypothetical]
   - **Follow-up:** "What were you trying to accomplish?"
   - **Follow-up:** "What made that hard/easy?"
   - **Follow-up:** "How did you solve it?"

5. "Can you describe a time when [problem your product solves] was particularly painful?"
   - [Listen for: emotion, workarounds, impact]
   - **Follow-up:** "How often does that happen?"
   - **Follow-up:** "What's the cost when it does happen?" (time, money, frustration)

6. "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [process], what would it be?"
   - [Listen for: root cause, not just symptoms]
   - **Follow-up:** "Why that specifically?"
   - **Follow-up:** "What have you tried to fix it?"

7. "How do you currently solve [problem]? Walk me through your process step-by-step."
   - [Listen for: workarounds, hacks, multi-tool workflows]
   - **Follow-up:** "What's the most frustrating part of that process?"
   - **Follow-up:** "What would make it 10x better?"

### Jobs-to-be-Done Deep Dive (10 min)

**Understand motivation and context:**

8. "When you decided to start using [current solution], what were you hoping it would do for you?"
   - [Listen for: functional, emotional, social jobs]
   - **Follow-up:** "What were you using before?"
   - **Follow-up:** "What made you switch?"

9. "What does success look like when you're done with [task]?"
   - [Listen for: measurable outcomes, feeling states]
   - **Follow-up:** "How do you know you did a good job?"

10. "If [task] took twice as long, what would be the impact?"
    - [Listen for: urgency, business impact]

### Solution Exploration (5-10 min)

**Test assumptions and explore preferences:**

11. "If I told you we're building [brief solution description], what's your first reaction?"
    - [Listen for: enthusiasm, skepticism, confusion]
    - **Follow-up:** "What would make you try it?"
    - **Follow-up:** "What would make you skeptical?"

12. "What's the most important thing this would need to do for you to use it?"
    - [Listen for: must-haves vs. nice-to-haves]
    - **Follow-up:** "What could we skip in v1?"

13. "How much would saving [X time/money] be worth to you?"
    - [Listen for: willingness to pay, budget authority]

### Closing (5 min)

14. "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?"
    - [Often surfaces unexpected insights]

15. "Do you know anyone else who struggles with [problem] that I should talk to?"
    - [Snowball sampling for more interviews]

16. "Would you be interested in trying an early version when it's ready?"
    - [Gauge real interest; build beta list]

**Thank and close:**
"This was super helpful. I'll send you [incentive] within [timeframe]. Thanks again for your time!"

## Interview Best Practices

### The Five Whys

When you get a surface-level answer, ask "why?" multiple times to get to root cause.

**Example:**
- User: "The app is too slow."
- You: "What specifically feels slow?"
- User: "Loading my dashboard."
- You: "When does that matter most?"
- User: "When I'm on a call and need to pull up data quickly."
- You: "What happens when you can't get it quickly?"
- User: "I lose credibility with clients."

**Root insight:** Speed isn't about general performance; it's about real-time credibility in high-stakes moments.

### Listen for Stories, Not Opinions

**Bad question:** "Would you use a feature that does X?"  
**Good question:** "Tell me about the last time you needed to do X."

Opinions are unreliable. Behavior is data.

### Don't Lead the Witness

**Bad:** "Do you find X frustrating?"  
**Good:** "How do you feel about X?"

**Bad:** "Would you pay $50/month for this?"  
**Good:** "How much would saving 10 hours/month be worth to you?"

### Silence is Golden

After asking a question, **shut up**. Let the interviewee think. Count to 10 in your head if needed. The best insights often come after awkward pauses.

### Take Notes on Emotion

Note when users light up, get frustrated, laugh, or pause. Emotion indicates importance.

**Examples:**
- "She got visibly excited when talking about [feature]"
- "He sighed heavily when describing [process]"
- "Long pause before answering [question about budget]"

### Distinguish Between Stated vs. Revealed Preferences

- **Stated:** What people say they do/want
- **Revealed:** What they actually do

Trust behavior over statements. If someone says they'd pay $100/month but currently uses a free tool and has never paid for software, believe the behavior.

## After the Interview

### Immediate Debrief (5 min)

Right after the call, jot down:
- Top 3 insights
- Surprises
- Quotes (verbatim if possible)
- Follow-up questions

### Synthesis Across Interviews

After 5-10 interviews:
- Look for patterns (3+ people saying similar things)
- Identify outliers (unique insights from power users or edge cases)
- Map to Jobs-to-be-Done framework
- Update personas and problem statements
- Revise product hypotheses

### Red Flags

**Stop interviewing if:**
- No one cares about the problem (after 5+ interviews)
- Current solutions are "good enough"
- You're not hearing consistent patterns (very fragmented problem space)

**These are signals to pivot or kill the idea early.**

## Sample Interview Script

[Modify based on your product space]

**Product:** [Project management tool]

---

**Opening:**  
"Thanks for joining. I'm working on better ways to manage projects, and I'd love to learn about your experience. There are no right answers—just want to understand your world. Cool if I record?"

**Background:**  
"Tell me about your role. What projects are you managing right now?"

**Problem Discovery:**  
"Walk me through the last time you kicked off a new project. What's the first thing you did? ... Then what? ... What was hard about that?"

"Describe a project that went off the rails. What happened? When did you first realize it was going sideways? What did you do?"

**Jobs-to-be-Done:**  
"When you're planning a project, what does 'done well' look like? How do you know you nailed it?"

"If project planning took 10x longer, what would break?"

**Solution Exploration:**  
"If I told you we're building a tool that auto-generates project plans from a brief, what's your reaction? What would make you try it? What would you worry about?"

**Closing:**  
"Anything I should have asked? Know anyone else juggling crazy projects I should talk to?"
